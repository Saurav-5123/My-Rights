Right to Freedom of Religion

Allows freedom of religion and religious practices.

The Right to Freedom of Religion is a fundamental right granted to every citizen of India, regardless of their faith. Enshrined in Articles 25 to 28 of the Indian Constitution, this right ensures that India remains a secular state, where individuals are free to practice, profess, and propagate any religion of their choice—or none at all.

This right promotes religious harmony, safeguards individual conscience, and prevents state interference in matters of personal faith, within the bounds of public order and morality.

Key Provisions under the Right to Freedom of Religion:

Article 25 – Freedom of Conscience and Free Profession, Practice, and Propagation of Religion

  • Every person has the right to freely profess, practice, and propagate their religion.
  • Includes the right to observe religious rituals, wear religious attire, and preach one’s faith.
  • Subject to reasonable restrictions related to public order, morality, and health.
  • The State can regulate or restrict economic, financial, or political activities associated with religious practice.

Article 26 – Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

  • Establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes.
  • Manage their own affairs in matters of religion.
  • Own and acquire property.
  • Administer such property according to law.

Article 27 – Freedom from Taxation for Promotion of a Religion

No person shall be forced to pay any taxes if the proceeds are specifically used for promoting or maintaining any particular religion or religious denomination.

Article 28 – Freedom as to Attendance at Religious Instruction

  • No religious instruction shall be provided in educational institutions wholly maintained by State funds.
  • Religious instruction is allowed in institutions administered by the State but established under endowments or trusts requiring such instruction.
  • Attendance in religious classes in such institutions is voluntary.

Scope and Limitations

While the Right to Religion is broad, it is not absolute. The State may intervene in the following scenarios:

  • To ensure public safety, order, morality, or health.
  • To bring social reform, such as banning untouchability or sati.
  • To regulate religious conversions if done through force, fraud, or inducement.
  • To apply secular laws in civil matters (e.g., marriage, divorce, adoption).

Importance of the Right to Religion:

  • Upholds secularism by maintaining state neutrality in religious matters.
  • Protects minority faiths and ensures equality among all religious groups.
  • Encourages interfaith harmony and peaceful coexistence.
  • Prevents religious persecution and state favoritism.
