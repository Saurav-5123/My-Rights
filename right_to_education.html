Right to Education

Ensures free and compulsory education for children.

The Right to Education (RTE) is a Fundamental Right under Article 21A of the Indian Constitution. It guarantees free and compulsory education to all children aged 6 to 14 years in India. This right aims to ensure that no child is left behind due to economic or social barriers.

Introduced through the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002, this right reflects India’s commitment to universal education and lays the foundation for a more informed, empowered, and equitable society.

Article 21A – Right to Education

Text:
“The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine.”

Key Highlights:

  • Free education: No child is required to pay any fee or charges to attend school.
  • Compulsory education: It is the State’s responsibility to ensure that every child in this age group is enrolled and attends school.
  • Applies to government as well as aided and unaided private schools (with certain conditions).

The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act)

To implement Article 21A, the RTE Act, 2009 was passed. It defines:

  • Norms and standards for schools
  • Infrastructure requirements
  • Teacher-student ratio
  • No detention and no corporal punishment policies
  • 25% reservation in private unaided schools for children from economically weaker sections and disadvantaged groups

Key Features of the Right to Education:

  • Universal Access
  • Quality Standards
  • Inclusive Education
  • Child-Centric Approach
  • Teacher Qualifications

Importance of the Right to Education:

  • Empowers children to build their future
  • Helps eliminate child labour, poverty, and illiteracy
  • Promotes equality by providing equal opportunities regardless of caste, gender, or class
  • Strengthens the democratic fabric of the country through an educated citizenry

Challenges in Implementation:

  • Dropout rates in certain areas due to economic or social reasons
  • Shortage of trained teachers in rural schools
  • Poor infrastructure in remote schools
  • Monitoring and accountability issues in private and government institutions
